Hyperlite Glide FPV Quadcopter Build

My newest and best FPV Quadcopter build thats using KababFPV's new Hyperlite Glide frame and the amazing Xing 2208 motors.

Recently KababFPV released this new frame, the Hyperlite Glide . I ordered it from PyroDrone, received it in the post and began to build it. To build it, I decided to use high quality components and used the recommended 20x20 Talon F7 flight controller I positioned towards the front of the quadcopter to minimise electrical noise and inteference. I wanted a high quality BLHeli32 ESC and ended up going with the T-Motor 45A 4in1 ESC as it has proven itself to be a reliable ESC. On top of the ESC I wanted to mount a VTX that had Smartaudio or Tramp. Originally I planned to use a Rush Tank VTX but due to them being sold out everywhere I used the TX805 which has Smartaudio and a large 800mW Output. For the FPV camera I went with the Foxeer Predator V3 with its super low latency and the fact that I pefer Foxeer FPV cameras over Runcam and Caddx cameras. At the back of the frame I wanted to mount the capacitor for the ESC and my R-XSR receiver with the antennas running into antenna tubes. The Hyperlite Glide frame and the Xing 2208 1800Kv motors make this quadcopter really stand out from others and allow it to really excel in freestyle and proximity flying.

After contructing the frame, I began to work on the layout of the components within the quadcopter, positioning the flight controller at the front of the quadcopter and allowing the wiring to run underneath it for a neater build. I decided to mount the VTX on its side to allow for the video antenna to run directly towards the back of the quadcopter reducing inteference with other components. The ESC board was mounted forward and to do this I ran the XT60 connector around the back of the frame. The motor wires were routed around the standoffs and connected onto the ESC from the inside reducing the risk of breaking the solder joints looking nicer. At the back of the frame, I used heatshrink and zipties to hold down the receiver and the capacitor that was mounted on WaLF's CapCap and ran the receivers antennas around the standoffs and through antenna tubes.

Once I decided on the layout of the components I began to solder wires to the flight controller from the VTX, FPV camera and receiver. To connect the flight controller to the ESC a wire harness was provided with the ESC, however it was the wrong style and I had to manually change it which was the hardest part of the build as the custom wire hardness I ordered from PyroDrone wasnt shipped and the shipping to Australia was expensive so rather than ship the $1 wire hardness for $40 I received a refund. Although the XT60 connector is usually the hardest thing to solder, it was very easy was I used high quality 63/37 TBS Solder rather than cheap solder. To connect the motor wires to the ESC, I routed the wires around the standoffs and cut the wires to length before soldering them.

I finished up the soldering and began to connect the wires to their respective component, screwing it in and securing it. The capacitor and the receiver were put into heatshrink and firmly ziptied into place. As I connected each component, I tested them through Betaflight to ensure they all functioned and were installed correctly. Everything was connected to 5V except for the VTX which was connected directly to the battery. The FPV camera was secured onto its mounts with 2 screws, all 4 motors were screwed in with locktite on the screws and the ESC and flight controller were locked in place with locknuts.

To finish off the build, I attached the antenna tubes and secured them with zipties and hot glue and placed the reciver antennas inside. To mount a better antenna than the MMCX whip antenna I used a MMCX to SMA connector and then desgined and 3D printed a SMA antenna mount to mount the better Emax pagoda video antenna. After testing the Quadcopter, programming the BLHeli32 ESCs and flashing the newest version Betaflight onto it, I gave it a test flight and everything functioned. I attached the top plate of the frame onto the standoffs and secured the GoPro mount to finalise the build and took it to the park to fly.
